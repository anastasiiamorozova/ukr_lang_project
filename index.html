Language Renaissance

How the full-scale war has pushed Ukrainians to abandon Russian language

The active phase of Russia's war against Ukraine has prompted more and more Ukrainians to speak their state language. Many of them have minimized or abandoned Russian language, as well as everything Russian.
Centuries of persecution of Ukrainian speakers in the Russian Empire, the Soviet Union and present-day Russia have not passed without a trace. And even with the independence of Ukraine, a huge number of people continued to speak the familiar Russian language.

However, the Orange Revolution, the Revolution of Dignity, the annexation of Crimea, the war in the east, the full-scale invasion have affected the desire of Ukrainians to continue speaking Russian.

By Anastasiia Morozova

Julianna Skibitskaya: "I don't communicate in Russian anymore"

Journalist Yulianna Skibitskaya lives and works in Kiev. She spent her childhood in the mid-90s in Zaporizhzhya - in a Russian-speaking environment. In high school, the girl's educational institution switched to the Ukrainian language of teaching. However, she still had to finish her studies according to the old version of textbooks - in Russian.

"For quite a long time I had as many lessons of Russian language and literature as lessons of Ukrainian, - says Yulianna. - You could say that all my childhood and for quite a long time in my youth I was brought up in a Russian-speaking environment, and I probably only had a couple of friends from Zaporizhzhya who spoke Ukrainian."

At the height of the Revolution of Dignity in 2013, she traveled to Kiev to express her position. At that time, not only residents of the city, but also people who had specially arrived from other regions came to Maidan and the streets of the capital to protest. Yulianna was among them.

"In Kiev, I didn't have the feeling that it was a completely different world. I do not remember that Kiev then was strongly Ukrainian-speaking, - recalls the journalist. - I had friends there, at work, somewhere else - and they were all Russian-speaking. I was brought up in Russian, I did not want to give it up.

Over the years, more and more Ukrainian-speaking acquaintances appeared in Yulianna's entourage and speaking Ukrainian became a habit, although there was no goal to stop speaking Russian. It didn't appear until the day of the full-scale invasion on February 24, 2022.

Yulia Khodyunova: "When I speak a lot of Ukrainian, I even get physically tired"

According to the last Ukrainian census in 2001, up to 4% of the population in the West of the country considered Russian to be their native language. Pensioner Yulia Khodyunova from Rivne belongs to this small group. However, after the full-scale invasion, she switched completely to Ukrainian. Her mother comes from the Kharkiv region. When she was young, she moved to Siberia to earn a living. There she met her future husband and had children. From an early age, her mother instilled in Yulia a love for Ukrainian culture, but the family spoke Russian. Ukrainian words were remembered only from folk songs. Her mother sang them to her exclusively in Ukrainian.

Later Yulia moved to Ukraine to work at the Rovno nuclear power plant. In those years it was possible to get an apartment for working there.

While in 2018 in Ternopil, Ivano-Frankivsk and Lviv regions, the vast majority of Internet queries were in Ukrainian, in Volyn and Rivne regions quite a few people spoke Russian. However, already by 2023, the number of Google queries in Ukrainian exceeded the number of queries in Russian by 3-4 times.

In public groups in social networks there were almost no messages of residents of Ternopil, Ivano-Frankivsk, Lviv, Volyn and Rivne regions in Russian. But in the first months of the invasion, the use of the Russian language increased sharply in the regions - then residents of eastern regions of Ukraine evacuated to the west of the country. In March and April 2022, the share of Russian language in these regions grew, and later went down again.

After the full-scale invasion, Yulia decided to completely abandon Russian in communication. However, in such a Ukrainian-speaking region, it was not easy for Yulia to find opportunities to learn the language with the help of specialists. According to her, there were mostly English language courses in Rivne, and similar courses for learning Ukrainian could not be found immediately.

"After the first victims, I wanted to change my place of birth. But this is my personal story. Yes, I was born in Russia. What can you do about it now? I myself began to be corrupted by my speech. I guess I've reached a point of no return, says the woman. - I can't say that twenty lessons have passed and I'm suddenly fluent in Ukrainian. When I speak a lot of Ukrainian, I even get physically tired. But this is the only way to improve the language."

Viktor Kachanov: "They asked me not to agonize and switch to Russian"

Pensioner Viktor Kachanov was born and lives in Dnipro. He is a volunteer and photographer, as well as an active participant of the "United" conversation club. Back in the days of Maidan, in 2013, his friends began to switch to Ukrainian. With the beginning of the war in the east, even more of them preferred Ukrainian to Russian. However, then Viktor did not dare to switch to the state language. The point of no return was the Russian invasion in 2022.

Viktor explains, he studied in a Soviet-era school. At that time, his school did not teach Ukrainian. In conversation classes, he was energized by the mutual support. "No one says you're bad at something. Everyone tries to help each other," the man shares. His old acquaintances and some of his relatives did not immediately believe that Viktor had seriously decided to switch to Ukrainian.

"They asked me not to agonize and switch to Russian. However, I did not make concessions," recalls a participant of the "United" conversation club. - Moments of personal attitude - desire and language stability - are very important. At first, I formed a vocabulary on everyday and professional topics. Waking up in the morning, I described my actions in Ukrainian and destroyed the switch to Russian. I changed the language of all gadgets and social networks, read and listened to audiobooks in Ukrainian".

Now Viktor Kachanov finds it difficult to communicate in Russian. He urges not to be afraid of making mistakes, to speak Surzhyk, to study Ukrainian history and the formation of the nation. The man is sure that until a person himself realizes that he needs it, no one can force him.

According to data in the social networks Telegram and Facebook, in the year since the invasion, the use of Ukrainian language in relation to Russian has doubled - from 22.3% to 44%. If before the invasion the share of Russian-language messages amounted to about 78% of messages, two years later it has decreased to 56%. This is confirmed by the results of a sociological survey in the Kyiv Digital app: today 86% of Kyiv residents consider Ukrainian their native language, but only 44% of Kyiv residents use it in everyday life.

Do people write the same language they think?

Independent creative Marina Chernyavskaya also sees a commercial problem in the Ukrainianization of content on the Internet. She now works on a freelance basis, helping businesses to build their marketing and PR strategies. Until 2012, Marina worked in the Ukrainian branch of a Russian advertising agency. For the startups she worked with, the Russian language was always more advantageous, because this way they could reach not only customers or users from Ukraine and Russia, but also from countries where Russian is quite common among the population, such as Kazakhstan.

In her opinion, the big changes in the marketing and content industry on the Internet occurred due to the fact that Russian businesses left the market, and advertising agencies stopped focusing on two countries at once. At the same time, it was primarily the government's initiatives at the legislative level that led to real results, followed by changes in the consciousness of the population.

"The only radical noticeable fact is the law on language. Not because we have become more conscious, but because we can be fined for a sign in Russian. Television and radio advertisements were in Ukrainian, but Internet advertisements, which are more popular and effective, remained in Russian."

The law that Marina recalls was signed by President Petro Poroshenko in 2019. It obliged all advertisers and media on the Internet to use the Ukrainian language, without restricting the existence of the Russian version of the website. Thus any outdoor advertising in public places and advertising on the Internet is obliged to be published only in the Ukrainian language.

"Now the attitude has changed not only to the product, but also to the market as a whole, we started to look with more respect at what we do ourselves, and to look less at Moscow and at Moscow salaries, although this continued, because we are traumatized by it.

Despite the state's attempts to change the language habits of the population, before the invasion almost half of Ukrainian families spoke Russian in everyday life. Now the stumbling block in the language debate is the Russian language option on selling websites.

Most popular queries on Google have the same trend - the use of Russian is decreasing, Ukrainian is increasing. Most often in Ukrainian people are looking for congratulations, including birthday greetings. However, some queries such as "buy a car" or "iPhone price" related to sales are not really so dynamic.
